# Glossary: Clinical and Medical Terms

> Auxiliary reference for understanding the clinical terminology used throughout the research paper.

---

## Treatment Approaches

### Adjuvant Therapy
Treatment given **after** the primary treatment (usually surgery) to reduce the risk of cancer returning. For example, chemotherapy given after breast cancer surgery to eliminate any remaining microscopic cancer cells.

### Neoadjuvant Therapy
Treatment given **before** the primary treatment (usually surgery) to shrink the tumor, making surgery easier or possible. For example, chemotherapy given before surgery for locally advanced breast cancer.

### Combination Chemotherapy Regimens
Standardized protocols that combine multiple drugs, each attacking cancer through different mechanisms to improve efficacy and reduce resistance. Common regimens mentioned in the paper:
- **MOPP:** Mechlorethamine, Oncovin (vincristine), Procarbazine, Prednisone -- first curative regimen for Hodgkin lymphoma
- **CHOP:** Cyclophosphamide, Hydroxydaunorubicin (doxorubicin), Oncovin (vincristine), Prednisone -- standard for non-Hodgkin lymphoma
- **FOLFOX:** Folinic acid (leucovorin), Fluorouracil, Oxaliplatin -- standard for colorectal cancer

### Chemo-Immunotherapy
A combination strategy using traditional chemotherapy alongside immunotherapy (typically checkpoint inhibitors). Chemotherapy can enhance immunotherapy by releasing tumor antigens and increasing immune cell infiltration. **KEYNOTE-189** (pembrolizumab + chemotherapy) is a landmark trial in non-small cell lung cancer.

### Precision Oncology
An approach to cancer treatment where therapy is selected based on the **molecular profile** of a patient's tumor rather than just the tumor's location in the body. This involves genomic sequencing to identify actionable mutations (e.g., EGFR, ALK, BRAF) and matching patients with the most effective targeted therapies.

### Tumor-Agnostic Approval
An FDA approval for a drug based on a **biomarker** rather than the type of cancer. This means the drug can be used for any cancer type that has the specified biomarker. Examples: pembrolizumab for MSI-H/dMMR tumors (regardless of whether it's colon, uterine, or another cancer).

## Cancer Types (Abbreviations)

### CML (Chronic Myeloid Leukemia)
A slow-growing blood cancer of the bone marrow involving overproduction of white blood cells. Driven by the **BCR-ABL fusion protein**. Imatinib transformed CML from a fatal disease to a manageable chronic condition with >90% five-year survival.

### ALL (Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia)
An aggressive blood cancer where the bone marrow produces too many immature lymphocytes (blast cells). Most common childhood cancer. CAR-T therapy (tisagenlecleucel) achieves complete remission in 50--90% of relapsed/refractory cases.

### B-ALL (B-cell Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia)
A subtype of ALL arising from B-cell precursors. The cancer cells typically express **CD19**, making them a prime target for CD19-directed CAR-T cells and bispecific antibodies like blinatumomab.

### NSCLC (Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer)
The most common type of lung cancer (~85% of cases), including adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma. Important because it frequently harbors **actionable mutations** (EGFR, ALK, ROS1, KRAS) that can be targeted with specific drugs.

### HER2+ Breast Cancer
Breast cancer in which the tumor cells overexpress the HER2 protein due to HER2 gene amplification. Represents ~15--20% of breast cancers. Once one of the most aggressive subtypes, outcomes dramatically improved with HER2-targeted therapies (trastuzumab, pertuzumab, T-DM1, trastuzumab deruxtecan).

### ER+ Breast Cancer (Estrogen Receptor-Positive)
Breast cancer where tumor growth is driven by estrogen signaling through the estrogen receptor. The most common subtype (~70% of breast cancers). Treated with hormonal therapies (tamoxifen, aromatase inhibitors) and CDK4/6 inhibitors.

## Clinical Outcomes

### Complete Remission (CR)
The disappearance of all detectable signs of cancer after treatment. Does not necessarily mean the cancer is cured -- microscopic cancer cells may remain. In CAR-T therapy, CR rates of 50--90% are observed in blood cancers.

### Relapsed/Refractory Disease
- **Relapsed:** Cancer that has returned after a period of remission
- **Refractory:** Cancer that does not respond to initial treatment

Patients with relapsed/refractory disease are often candidates for newer therapies (CAR-T, bispecific antibodies) after standard treatments have failed.

### Durable Response
A treatment response that lasts for an extended period (typically >1--2 years). A hallmark of checkpoint immunotherapy -- some patients achieve durable responses lasting 5+ years, suggesting a subset may be effectively cured.

### Therapeutic Window
The range between the minimum effective dose and the dose that causes unacceptable toxicity. First-generation chemotherapy has a **narrow therapeutic window** (effective doses are close to toxic doses), while targeted therapies generally have a wider window due to greater selectivity.

## Biomarkers

### Biomarker
A measurable indicator used to guide treatment decisions. In oncology:
- **Predictive biomarkers:** Indicate likelihood of response to a specific therapy (e.g., HER2 overexpression predicts response to trastuzumab)
- **Prognostic biomarkers:** Indicate likely disease outcome regardless of treatment

### PD-L1 Expression
The level of PD-L1 protein on tumor cells or immune cells in the tumor microenvironment, measured by immunohistochemistry (IHC). Higher PD-L1 expression generally correlates with better response to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy, but it is an **imperfect predictor** -- some PD-L1-negative patients respond, and some PD-L1-positive patients do not.

### HRD (Homologous Recombination Deficiency)
A tumor characteristic indicating defective DNA repair via homologous recombination (not limited to BRCA mutations). HRD-positive tumors are sensitive to **PARP inhibitors** and platinum-based chemotherapy. Measured by genomic tests that assess loss of heterozygosity, telomeric allelic imbalance, and large-scale state transitions.

## Toxicity Terms

### Myelosuppression
Suppression of bone marrow activity, leading to reduced production of blood cells:
- **Neutropenia:** Low white blood cells (increased infection risk)
- **Anemia:** Low red blood cells (fatigue, shortness of breath)
- **Thrombocytopenia:** Low platelets (increased bleeding risk)

The most common dose-limiting toxicity of conventional chemotherapy.

### Mucositis
Painful inflammation and ulceration of the mucous membranes lining the digestive tract, from the mouth to the intestines. A frequent side effect of chemotherapy and radiation, as mucosal cells divide rapidly and are highly susceptible to cytotoxic drugs.

### Nephrotoxicity
Kidney damage caused by a drug. Cisplatin is particularly nephrotoxic, which led to the development of **carboplatin** (a second-generation platinum agent) with reduced kidney toxicity.

### Neurotoxicity
Damage to the nervous system caused by a drug or therapy. In oncology:
- **Chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy:** Caused by vincristine, oxaliplatin, paclitaxel
- **ICANS (Immune Effector Cell-Associated Neurotoxicity Syndrome):** A specific form of neurotoxicity seen with CAR-T cell therapy, causing confusion, tremor, and in severe cases, cerebral edema
