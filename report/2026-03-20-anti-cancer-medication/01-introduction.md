# 1. Introduction

Cancer remains one of the leading causes of morbidity and mortality worldwide. The history of cancer pharmacotherapy spans from ancient surgical interventions to the modern era of molecular medicine. A pivotal moment occurred after the Second World War with the discovery of cytotoxic antitumor drugs and the birth of chemotherapy. Since then, advances in molecular biology, genomics, and immunology have driven successive waves of innovation, each representing a distinct "generation" of anti-cancer medication.

## Generational Framework

This paper organizes anti-cancer drugs into four generations based on their historical emergence, mechanism of action, and therapeutic philosophy:

| Generation | Era | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| First | 1940s--1990s | Cytotoxic chemotherapy |
| Second | 1990s--2010s | Molecularly targeted therapy |
| Third | 2010s--present | Immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors, cytokines) |
| Fourth | 2017--present | Adoptive cell therapies (CAR-T), bispecific antibodies, next-generation platforms |

## Scope

Each generation is examined in terms of:
- **Historical context** -- The scientific breakthroughs that enabled each wave
- **Mechanism of action** -- How drugs in each generation work at the molecular level
- **Representative drugs** -- Key agents and their clinical applications
- **Clinical impact** -- How each generation changed patient outcomes
- **Limitations** -- Challenges that drove the development of the next generation

## Rationale

Understanding this generational progression provides critical context for:
1. Current treatment paradigms and combination strategies
2. Ongoing clinical trial design
3. Future directions in oncology drug development
4. The shift from "one-size-fits-all" to precision medicine
