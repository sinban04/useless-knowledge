# Cancer Types and Their Treatment Characteristics

> This document explains how different cancer types differ from one another in terms of biology, behavior, and how these differences shape treatment decisions. It is designed to complement the generational drug overview in the main research paper.

---

## Why Cancer Types Are Treated Differently

Cancer is not a single disease -- it is a broad term for over 100 distinct diseases that share one core feature: uncontrolled cell growth. However, the **cell of origin**, the **organ it arises in**, the **mutations it carries**, and the **tumor's relationship with the immune system** all vary enormously across cancer types. These differences directly determine:

- Which generation of drug is most likely to work
- Whether a biomarker test is needed before treatment
- How aggressive the treatment can be
- What resistance mechanisms to expect

The table below summarizes major cancer types grouped by category, with key treatment characteristics for each.

---

## 1. Hematological Cancers (Blood and Bone Marrow)

Cancers of the blood, bone marrow, and lymphatic system. These are generally **more responsive** to systemic therapies (chemotherapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy) than solid tumors because the cancer cells are already circulating in the bloodstream and are directly accessible to drugs.

### 1.1 Leukemias

Leukemias arise from the bone marrow and produce large numbers of immature or abnormal blood cells.

| Feature | CML | ALL | AML | CLL |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| **Full name** | Chronic Myeloid Leukemia | Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia | Acute Myeloid Leukemia | Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia |
| **Cell of origin** | Myeloid progenitor | Lymphoid progenitor (B or T) | Myeloid progenitor | Mature B cell |
| **Speed** | Slow (chronic) | Fast (acute) | Fast (acute) | Slow (chronic) |
| **Key driver mutation** | BCR-ABL (Philadelphia chromosome) | Various (e.g., BCR-ABL, MLL rearrangement) | FLT3, NPM1, IDH1/2 | del(17p), TP53, IGHV mutations |
| **1st-line therapy** | TKI (imatinib/dasatinib) | Combination chemo + TKI if BCR-ABL+ | Intensive chemotherapy (7+3) | Targeted (BTK inhibitors: ibrutinib) |
| **Role of immunotherapy** | Limited | CAR-T (tisagenlecleucel) for relapsed | Limited; AML-targeting bispecifics emerging | Anti-CD20 (obinutuzumab, rituximab) |
| **Treatability** | Excellent (imatinib: >90% 5-yr survival) | Good in pediatric (~90% cure rate) | Variable (poorer in elderly) | Incurable but manageable |

**Why they respond well to targeted therapy:** Blood cancers often have a single dominant **driver mutation** (like BCR-ABL in CML) that is the primary force sustaining the cancer -- blocking it is highly effective. Solid tumors are typically more genetically heterogeneous.

---

### 1.2 Lymphomas

Lymphomas arise from lymphocytes and grow in the lymph nodes, spleen, or other lymphoid tissue.

| Feature | Hodgkin Lymphoma | Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL) | Follicular Lymphoma | T-Cell Lymphoma |
|---------|-----------------|--------------------------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Cell of origin** | Reed-Sternberg B cell | Mature B cell | Mature B cell (follicular) | Mature T cell |
| **Speed** | Varies | Aggressive | Indolent (slow) | Aggressive |
| **Key feature** | CD30 expression; PD-L1 overexpression | CD20 expression; MYC rearrangements | BCL2 rearrangement | Heterogeneous; complex |
| **Standard therapy** | ABVD chemo ± brentuximab vedotin (ADC) | R-CHOP (rituximab + CHOP) | Watch-and-wait or R-chemo | Chemo (CHOP-based) ± brentuximab |
| **Immunotherapy** | Anti-PD-1 (nivolumab, pembrolizumab) highly effective | CAR-T (axi-cel, liso-cel) for relapsed | Anti-CD20 mAbs | Mogamulizumab (anti-CCR4) |
| **Curability** | >85% cure with chemo-based regimens | ~60% cured with R-CHOP; CAR-T can rescue relapsed | Incurable but long survival | Poorer prognosis |

**Notable:** Hodgkin lymphoma was one of the first cancers cured by combination chemotherapy (MOPP regimen, 1960s) and is now highly curable. It also overexpresses **PD-L1**, making it exceptionally responsive to anti-PD-1 therapy.

---

### 1.3 Multiple Myeloma

A cancer of **plasma cells** (antibody-producing B cells) in the bone marrow.

| Feature | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Driver biology** | Clonal plasma cell expansion; BCMA expression; relies on bone marrow microenvironment |
| **Key mutations** | t(4;14), t(14;16), del(17p), KRAS/NRAS mutations |
| **Standard therapy** | Triplet/quadruplet combinations: proteasome inhibitors (bortezomib) + immunomodulatory drugs (lenalidomide) + anti-CD38 mAbs (daratumumab) |
| **4th-gen therapies** | BCMA-targeted CAR-T (ciltacabtagene autoleucel), bispecifics (teclistamab), and ADCs (belantamab mafodotin) |
| **Treatability** | Incurable but dramatically improving survival; median OS now >7--10 years with modern regimens |

## 2. Solid Tumors: Lung Cancer

Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death worldwide. It is split into two fundamentally different diseases based on pathology and treatment.

| Feature | Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer (NSCLC) | Small Cell Lung Cancer (SCLC) |
|---------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Frequency** | ~85% of lung cancers | ~15% of lung cancers |
| **Key subtypes** | Adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, large cell carcinoma | Limited or extensive stage |
| **Driver mutations** | EGFR (10--15% Western, 30--40% Asian), ALK, ROS1, KRAS G12C, BRAF V600E, MET, RET, NTRK | RB1 + TP53 loss (almost universal); very high TMB |
| **Standard treatment** | TKI if driver mutation present; chemo-immunotherapy if not; pembrolizumab monotherapy if PD-L1 >50% | Platinum-based chemo (carboplatin/etoposide) + atezolizumab |
| **Immunotherapy response** | PD-L1+ tumors respond well; driver mutation-positive tumors often do NOT respond | Modest; adds modest benefit to chemo |
| **Curability** | Stage I--II: potentially curable with surgery ± adjuvant therapy; Stage IV: not curable but dramatically prolonged with TKIs | Poor prognosis; median survival ~12--18 months even with treatment |
| **Key challenge** | Acquired resistance (EGFR T790M, C797S; ALK resistance mutations) | Rapid development of chemo resistance; neuroendocrine biology |

**Why EGFR mutation matters:** Patients with EGFR-mutant NSCLC respond dramatically better to EGFR TKIs (osimertinib) than to chemotherapy, while patients with wild-type EGFR do not benefit from TKIs at all. **Testing is essential before starting treatment.**

## 3. Solid Tumors: Breast Cancer

Breast cancer is not one disease -- it is at least four molecularly distinct subtypes with very different treatment strategies.

| Subtype | Definition | Treatment Approach | Prognosis |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| **HR+/HER2−** (Luminal A/B) | Estrogen/progesterone receptor positive, HER2 normal. Most common (~70%) | Endocrine therapy (aromatase inhibitors, tamoxifen) + CDK4/6 inhibitors (palbociclib, ribociclib). Chemotherapy reserved for high-risk cases | Generally favorable; 5-yr survival >90% for early stage |
| **HER2+** | HER2 gene amplified/overexpressed (~15--20%) | Anti-HER2 targeted therapy (trastuzumab, pertuzumab, T-DM1, trastuzumab deruxtecan) + chemotherapy | Once the worst subtype; now among the most treatable |
| **Triple-Negative (TNBC)** | ER−, PR−, HER2− (~15--20%). Associated with BRCA mutations | Chemotherapy remains backbone; pembrolizumab + chemo for PD-L1+ tumors; olaparib for BRCA-mutated; sacituzumab govitecan (ADC, Trop-2 target) | Historically worst prognosis; improving with immunotherapy and ADCs |
| **HER2-low** | Low HER2 expression (not amplified) -- newly recognized category (~50--60% of HER2− tumors) | Trastuzumab deruxtecan (Enhertu) showed transformative efficacy | Paradigm shift: T-DXd expanded HER2-directed therapy to this previously undertreated group |

**Why subtyping is critical:** The same surgical appearance of breast cancer requires completely different drugs depending on receptor status. Giving chemotherapy alone to HR+ patients misses the opportunity for endocrine therapy, while giving endocrine therapy alone to HER2+ patients would be inadequate.

## 4. Solid Tumors: Colorectal Cancer (CRC)

| Feature | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Key molecular subtypes** | MSI-H/dMMR (~15% of all, ~5% metastatic) vs. MSS (microsatellite stable) |
| **Key mutations** | KRAS/NRAS (40--50%, predicts lack of response to anti-EGFR therapy), BRAF V600E (~10%), HER2 amplification (~3%) |
| **Standard therapy** | FOLFOX or FOLFIRI ± bevacizumab (anti-VEGF) ± cetuximab/panitumumab (anti-EGFR, only if RAS wild-type) |
| **Immunotherapy** | Pembrolizumab approved for MSI-H/dMMR CRC (first-line for metastatic); **MSS tumors are essentially resistant to checkpoint inhibitors** |
| **Why MSS CRC doesn't respond to immunotherapy** | MSS tumors have low TMB, few neoantigens, and a "cold" (immunosuppressive) tumor microenvironment with few infiltrating T cells |
| **BRAF V600E subtype** | Aggressive; treated with BRAF inhibitor (encorafenib) + anti-EGFR antibody (binimetinib/cetuximab) |

**The RAS testing lesson:** Anti-EGFR antibodies (cetuximab, panitumumab) only work in **KRAS/NRAS wild-type** patients. Patients with RAS mutations see no benefit and are spared the cost and toxicity. This is one of the clearest examples of biomarker-directed treatment selection.

## 5. Solid Tumors: Melanoma

Melanoma is the cancer where immunotherapy had its most dramatic early successes.

| Feature | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Cell of origin** | Melanocytes (pigment-producing skin cells) |
| **Key mutations** | BRAF V600E/K (~50%), NRAS (~20%), NF1, wild-type BRAF |
| **Immunotherapy response** | Excellent -- highest response rates among all solid tumors to checkpoint inhibitors; durable long-term responses seen |
| **Targeted therapy** | BRAF + MEK inhibitors (dabrafenib + trametinib; vemurafenib + cobimetinib) for BRAF V600-mutated tumors |
| **Why it responds to immunotherapy** | High TMB (UV-induced mutations create many neoantigens); naturally immunogenic |
| **Standard first-line** | Pembrolizumab or nivolumab ± ipilimumab; BRAF-mutated patients: choice between targeted therapy and immunotherapy |
| **Curability** | Stage IV: 20--30% long-term survival with immunotherapy (unheard of before 2011); Stage III: adjuvant immunotherapy significantly reduces recurrence |

**The historic shift:** Before ipilimumab (2011), median survival for metastatic melanoma was ~9 months. Today, approximately 20--25% of metastatic melanoma patients achieve long-term survival exceeding 10 years with checkpoint immunotherapy.

## 6. Solid Tumors: Prostate Cancer

Prostate cancer is unique in being driven by **androgen (male hormone) signaling**, making it a target for hormonal manipulation.

| Feature | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Cell of origin** | Prostate epithelial cells |
| **Hormone dependence** | Most prostate cancers express the androgen receptor (AR) and are driven by testosterone signaling |
| **Key mutations** | BRCA1/2 (~12% of metastatic cases), AR amplification/mutation, CDK12, ATM |
| **Treatment stages** | Localized: surgery or radiation; castration-sensitive: androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) + docetaxel or abiraterone/enzalutamide; castration-resistant: see below |
| **Castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC)** | Disease that progresses despite low testosterone. Treated with abiraterone (CYP17A1 inhibitor), enzalutamide (AR antagonist), docetaxel, cabazitaxel |
| **PARP inhibitors** | Olaparib, rucaparib approved for BRCA/HRD-positive CRPC |
| **Immunotherapy** | Limited overall; sipuleucel-T (dendritic cell vaccine); pembrolizumab for MSI-H or TMB-high cases |
| **Radioligand therapy** | Lutetium-177 PSMA (Pluvicto) for PSMA-positive CRPC -- the most significant recent advance |

---

## 7. Solid Tumors: Ovarian Cancer

| Feature | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Key subtypes** | High-grade serous (most common, ~70%), clear cell, mucinous, low-grade serous |
| **Driver alterations** | BRCA1/2 mutation (~15% germline, ~7% somatic); HRD (~50% overall); TP53 (almost universal in HGSOC) |
| **Standard therapy** | Platinum-taxane chemotherapy (carboplatin + paclitaxel); PARP inhibitor maintenance after response |
| **PARP inhibitor role** | Olaparib, niraparib, rucaparib as maintenance therapy; most effective in BRCA-mutated patients; HRD-positive patients also benefit |
| **Immunotherapy** | Limited -- most ovarian tumors are "cold" and PD-L1 expression is low; trials ongoing |
| **Key challenge** | Most patients (~70%) achieve remission with first-line platinum chemotherapy, but ~80% relapse; platinum sensitivity/resistance determines subsequent options |

**Why BRCA mutation matters differently here:** In ovarian cancer, BRCA mutation is both a **risk factor** (inherited BRCA mutations explain ~10--15% of ovarian cancers) and a **treatment predictor** -- BRCA-mutated tumors respond better to both platinum chemotherapy and PARP inhibitors.

---

## 8. Solid Tumors: Pancreatic Cancer

Pancreatic cancer represents one of the most challenging malignancies in all of oncology.

| Feature | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Key mutation** | KRAS (>90% of cases) -- the most common oncogenic driver but historically undruggable; TP53, CDKN2A, SMAD4 |
| **Standard therapy** | FOLFIRINOX (5-FU + irinotecan + oxaliplatin) or gemcitabine + nab-paclitaxel; both are aggressive chemotherapy regimens |
| **Targeted therapy** | Erlotinib (EGFR inhibitor) has modest benefit; KRAS G12C/G12D inhibitors in trials; olaparib for BRCA-mutated |
| **Immunotherapy** | Largely resistant -- "cold" immunosuppressive TME; desmoplastic stroma (dense fibrous tissue) physically blocks immune cell infiltration |
| **Recent KRAS breakthrough** | KRAS G12C inhibitor (sotorasib, adagrasib) approved for NSCLC -- pancreatic KRAS G12D inhibitors now entering trials |
| **5-year survival** | ~12% overall; ~3% for metastatic disease |
| **Why it's so hard to treat** | Late diagnosis (no symptoms until advanced); dense desmoplastic stroma creates a physical barrier; near-universal KRAS mutation historically undruggable; immunosuppressive microenvironment |

| Cancer Type | Best Drug Generation | Biomarker Testing Required? | Immunotherapy Response | Key Drug Class |
|------------|---------------------|----------------------------|----------------------|----------------|
| **CML** | 2nd (TKI) | BCR-ABL | Minimal | TKI (imatinib) |
| **ALL** | 1st + 4th | BCR-ABL, CD19 | CAR-T (excellent) | Chemo + CAR-T |
| **AML** | 1st + 2nd | FLT3, IDH1/2 | Limited | Chemo ± targeted |
| **Hodgkin lymphoma** | 1st + 2nd + 3rd | CD30 | Excellent (anti-PD-1) | ABVD + brentuximab |
| **DLBCL** | 1st + 4th | CD20 | CAR-T (relapsed) | R-CHOP + CAR-T |
| **Multiple myeloma** | 2nd + 4th | BCMA, CD38 | Bispecifics/CAR-T | Triplet regimens + immunotherapy |
| **NSCLC (driver+)** | 2nd (TKI) | EGFR, ALK, KRAS, etc. | Poor (driver+ tumors) | EGFR/ALK/ROS1 TKI |
| **NSCLC (driver−)** | 1st + 3rd | PD-L1 | Moderate-excellent | Chemo-immunotherapy |
| **SCLC** | 1st + 3rd | None | Modest | Platinum-etoposide + atezo |
| **Breast (HR+)** | 2nd | ER, PR, CDK4/6 | Limited | Endocrine + CDK4/6 inhibitors |
| **Breast (HER2+)** | 2nd + 4th | HER2 | Limited | Anti-HER2 (trastuzumab, T-DXd) |
| **Breast (TNBC)** | 1st + 3rd + 4th | PD-L1, BRCA | Moderate | Chemo + pembrolizumab + ADC |
| **CRC (MSI-H)** | 3rd | MSI/MMR status | Excellent | Pembrolizumab |
| **CRC (MSS)** | 1st + 2nd | KRAS/NRAS, BRAF | Essentially none | FOLFOX/FOLFIRI + bevacizumab |
| **Melanoma** | 2nd + 3rd | BRAF V600 | Excellent | Anti-PD-1 ± anti-CTLA-4 |
| **Prostate** | 2nd (hormonal) | BRCA/HRD, PSMA | Limited | ADT + enzalutamide/abiraterone |
| **Ovarian** | 1st + 2nd | BRCA, HRD | Limited | Platinum-taxane + PARP inhibitor |
| **Pancreatic** | 1st | BRCA, KRAS G12C/D | Essentially none | FOLFIRINOX / gemcitabine |

## 10. Key Principles That Explain Treatment Differences

### Principle 1: "Hot" vs. "Cold" Tumors
Tumors with high mutation burden (melanoma, MSI-H CRC, SCLC) produce more neoantigens and attract more immune cells -- they are **"hot"** and respond well to immunotherapy. Tumors with low mutation burden, dense stroma, and few immune infiltrates (pancreatic, ovarian, MSS CRC) are **"cold"** and resist immunotherapy.

### Principle 2: One Driver vs. Many Drivers
Blood cancers like CML often have a single dominant driver (BCR-ABL) that is necessary for cancer survival -- block it and the cancer collapses. Pancreatic cancer has KRAS in >90% of cases but also multiple cooperating alterations, making single-agent targeted therapy insufficient.

### Principle 3: Tissue Architecture Matters
Hematological cancers circulate freely in the blood -- drugs reach them easily. Solid tumors are surrounded by a **tumor microenvironment** including dense stroma, abnormal blood vessels, and immunosuppressive cells that physically and biochemically impede drug penetration and immune cell infiltration.

### Principle 4: Hormonal Cancers Follow the Hormone
Prostate and breast cancers that express hormone receptors are fueled by sex hormones. Depriving them of hormone signaling (through ADT, aromatase inhibitors, antiestrogens) remains one of the most effective and well-tolerated cancer treatments, even in the era of immunotherapy.

### Principle 5: Biomarker-Guided Treatment Is Now Standard
Increasingly, it is not where the cancer is but **what molecular features it carries** that determines treatment. A lung cancer and a colon cancer with the same NTRK fusion are both treated with larotrectinib (tumor-agnostic approval). The shift from anatomical to molecular classification is the central trend of modern oncology.
