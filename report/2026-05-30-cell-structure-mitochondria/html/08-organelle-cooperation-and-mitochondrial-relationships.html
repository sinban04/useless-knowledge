Workflow page

Organelle cooperation and mitochondrial signaling

Real cells are not organelle lists. They are workflows: the nucleus writes instructions, ribosomes translate, mitochondria provide energy and status signals, membranes exchange cargo, lysosomes recycle, and the cytoskeleton positions everything.

Markdown chapter Next chapter

The Three Arrows That Are Easy To Mix Up

The confusing part is that mitochondria and the nucleus communicate in more than one way. These arrows happen at different time scales and move different things.

Evolution

Gene transfer

Over deep evolutionary time, genes from the ancestral mitochondrial endosymbiont moved into the host nuclear genome.

Moves: DNA across generations, not a normal daily signal.
Daily construction

Protein import

Nuclear genes are transcribed, cytosolic ribosomes translate them, and mitochondrial targeting/import systems bring the proteins into mitochondria.

Moves: mRNA out of nucleus, protein into mitochondria.
Real-time control

Retrograde signaling

Mitochondria report energy, redox, calcium, metabolite, folding, or damage state. Nuclear transcription changes in response.

Moves: signals and pathway activity, not routine mtDNA transfer.

What Does Not Normally Happen

The wrong picture is "mitochondrial DNA goes to the nucleus, then the nucleus transcribes it." That is not the everyday workflow in modern cells.

Workflow 1: Nuclear Gene To Mitochondrial Protein

This is the main daily construction route. The nucleus stores the gene; the cytosol makes the protein; mitochondria import the protein.

2

↓
3

↓
4
TOM/TIM importOuter- and inner-membrane translocases route the protein into the matrix, inner membrane, intermembrane space, or outer membrane.

AssemblyThe imported protein joins local mitochondrial products, cofactors, lipids, and partner proteins.

Workflow 2: Mitochondrial DNA Is Used Locally

Mitochondria still have a small genome. In humans it encodes 13 respiratory-chain protein subunits, 22 tRNAs, and 2 rRNAs. Those products are made inside mitochondria and combined with many imported nuclear-encoded proteins.

1
mtDNA in matrixThe mitochondrial genome is organized inside the mitochondrial matrix.
↓
2
Mitochondrial transcriptionMitochondrial transcription machinery makes mitochondrial RNAs.
↓
3
Mitochondrial translationMitochondrial ribosomes translate a small set of local respiratory-chain proteins.
↓
4
Complex assemblyLocal mtDNA-encoded proteins assemble with many imported nuclear-encoded proteins to form working oxidative-phosphorylation complexes.

Workflow 3: How Mitochondria Trigger Nuclear Transcription

This is the workflow you were asking about. Mitochondria usually trigger nuclear transcription by changing signals, not by sending their DNA to the nucleus.

1
Mitochondrial condition changesATP may fall, AMP may rise, ROS may increase, redox balance may shift, Ca2+ handling may change, metabolites may accumulate, protein folding may fail, or mtDNA may leak during severe damage.
↓
2
Cytosolic pathways sense the changeEnergy-stress, redox, calcium, metabolite, proteostasis, or innate immune pathways convert mitochondrial status into signaling activity.
↓
3
Transcription factors/coactivators changeSignaling changes the activity, location, or modification state of nuclear regulators.
↓
4
Nuclear transcription changesThe nucleus transcribes genes for repair, metabolism, antioxidant defense, mitochondrial biogenesis, protein import, autophagy, inflammation, or cell-fate decisions.
↓
5
Feedback returns to mitochondriaNew nuclear gene products are translated and routed back into cellular workflows, often including mitochondrial repair or turnover.

Workflow 4: ATP Where It Is Needed

Mitochondria make ATP, but local demand varies. The cytoskeleton positions mitochondria near the work.

Synapses

Vesicle cycling, ion pumps, transmitter release, and calcium buffering need local mitochondrial support.

Muscle fibers

Myosin contraction, Ca2+ cycling, and ion gradients create heavy ATP demand.

Secretory cells

Protein folding, vesicle traffic, membrane fusion, and ion pumps require ATP and calcium control.

1
Demand risesA region needs ATP or calcium buffering.
↓
2
Mitochondria move or anchorMicrotubule and actin systems position mitochondria near that demand.
↓
3
Local function improvesSecretion, contraction, synaptic release, migration, or division can proceed.

A damaged mitochondrion is not just inefficient. It can disturb redox balance, calcium handling, ATP production, and cell-death control.

Workflow 6: Membrane Contact Sites

Organelles can cooperate through close contact without fully fusing. ER-mitochondria contacts are especially important for lipid transfer, calcium signaling, and fission positioning.

ER-mitochondria

Lipid transfer, Ca2+ signaling, and fission coordination.

Mitochondria-lysosome

Quality-control and signaling contacts.

Nucleus-cytoskeleton

Mechanical coupling and position control.

Example: Secreting A Protein Hormone

No single organelle "does secretion." The cell does secretion through a coordinated route.

1
NucleusTranscribes the hormone gene into mRNA.
2
Ribosome and ERTranslation starts; signal peptide routes the protein to rough ER for folding.
3
Golgi and vesiclesThe Golgi modifies and packages the protein; vesicles move along cytoskeletal tracks.
4
Plasma membraneReceives and releases the vesicle contents.
5
Mitochondria and lysosomesMitochondria provide ATP/Ca2+ support; lysosomes recycle failed proteins or old parts.

Summary

The chapter's main idea is not "one organelle causes one function." The real model is routed work and feedback.

mtDNAMostly stays inside mitochondria and is used locally.
NucleusEncodes most mitochondrial proteins and receives mitochondrial status signals.
RibosomesTranslate nuclear-encoded mitochondrial proteins in the cytosol.
MitochondriaSupply ATP/metabolites and report stress state.
LysosomesRecycle damaged mitochondria through mitophagy.
