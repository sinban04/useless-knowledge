Mitochondria, self-recycling, protein state, and chromatin

A modern eukaryotic cell is best understood as a historical merger that became one operating system. Mitochondria retain bacterial ancestry, the nucleus now controls most mitochondrial construction, autophagy removes failed parts, proteins keep their state through targeting and quality control, and chromatin packages DNA as a regulated material rather than as a passive spool.

1. The Cell As A Historical Merger

The phrase "mitochondrion part and the other part coexist" is a strong intuition, but it needs one correction: mitochondria are not independent partners living beside the cell. They are descendants of a once-free-living bacterial lineage that became an organelle.

Best mental model A eukaryotic cell is not a bag containing a mitochondrion. It is a host-derived cellular system plus bacterial-descended mitochondria plus integration machinery that makes both sides depend on each other.
Host-derivedThe surrounding cell systemNucleus, cytosol, plasma membrane, ER, Golgi, lysosomes, cytoskeleton, most ribosomes, signaling pathways, and most gene regulation.
Bacterial-descendedThe mitochondrial systemDouble membrane, small genome, local ribosomes, respiratory-chain membrane, fission/fusion dynamics, and specialized energy conversion.
IntegrationThe bridge between themNuclear genes for mitochondrial proteins, cytosolic translation, TOM/TIM import, lipid exchange, cytoskeletal positioning, and lysosomal quality control.
CorrectionNot two equal organismsModern mitochondria cannot rebuild themselves from nothing and cannot live as their ancestor did. They grow and divide only inside the cellular environment.

What Stayed Bacterial, And What Became Host-Controlled?

The present cell carries both signatures at once. The bacterial ancestry explains mitochondrial membranes, DNA, ribosomes, and fission. The host takeover explains why most mitochondrial protein construction is now routed through the nucleus and cytosol.

Bacterial traces

  • Double membrane architecture.
  • Small mitochondrial genome.
  • Mitochondrial ribosomes and local translation.
  • Growth and division by fission from existing mitochondria.
  • Energy conversion across a specialized inner membrane.

Host control

  • Most mitochondrial proteins are encoded by nuclear genes.
  • Many mitochondrial proteins are translated by cytosolic ribosomes.
  • Import channels deliver proteins into mitochondrial compartments.
  • Lipid exchange and cytoskeleton positioning require the rest of the cell.
  • Damaged mitochondria can be removed by mitophagy.

Integrated result

  • The mitochondrion keeps enough autonomy to run local energy chemistry.
  • The nucleus keeps enough control to coordinate energy, growth, stress, and death decisions.
  • The boundary is historical, not a clean split between two living organisms.

2. Nuclear Genes Can Make Mitochondrial Proteins

The apparent paradox disappears when gene location and protein work location are separated. A mitochondrial protein is a protein that functions in mitochondria; it does not have to be encoded by mitochondrial DNA.

Protein category Gene location Protein workplace What this means
Mitochondrial DNA-encoded protein Mitochondrial genome Mitochondrion In humans, this is a small set of respiratory-chain protein subunits made inside mitochondria.
Nuclear DNA-encoded mitochondrial protein Nuclear genome Mitochondrion Most mitochondrial enzymes, structural proteins, import factors, ribosomal proteins, and DNA-maintenance proteins belong here.
Non-mitochondrial protein Usually nuclear genome Cytosol, nucleus, ER, Golgi, lysosome, membrane, secretion path, or another compartment Location is controlled by sequence signals, transport machinery, and retention/degradation systems.

The Route: Nuclear Gene To Mitochondrial Protein

A protein can be encoded in the nucleus and still become a mitochondrial protein because the protein carries address information and mitochondria have import machinery.

1
Nuclear DNA stores the geneThe gene is transcribed in the nucleus under nuclear regulatory control.
↓
2
mRNA exits through nuclear poresThe message is now in the cytosol, where cytosolic ribosomes can translate it.
↓
3
Cytosolic ribosome makes a precursor proteinMany mitochondrial proteins are synthesized outside mitochondria before import.
↓
4
Targeting signal identifies the destinationA mitochondrial targeting sequence or internal targeting information makes the protein recognizable to mitochondrial import receptors.
↓
5
TOM and TIM channels move the protein across membranesThe outer membrane TOM complex and inner membrane TIM complexes route the protein to the matrix, inner membrane, intermembrane space, or outer membrane.
↓
6
Assembly finishes inside the organelleImported proteins combine with locally made mitochondrial proteins, cofactors, lipids, and other subunits to build working mitochondrial systems.

How Did Mitochondrial Genes Move To The Nucleus?

Over evolutionary time, pieces of endosymbiont DNA could enter the host genome. Most transfers would be useless. A transfer became useful only if the nuclear copy could be expressed, targeted back to mitochondria, and coordinated with the remaining mitochondrial system.

A
DNA transferFragments from the ancestral mitochondrial genome entered the host nuclear lineage.
B
Nuclear expressionA transferred gene needed promoters, RNA processing, and expression timing that worked in the nucleus.
C
Protein targetingThe encoded protein needed a way back to mitochondria, often through acquisition or preservation of mitochondrial targeting information.
D
Functional replacementThe imported nuclear-encoded protein had to replace the organelle-encoded version without breaking respiratory complexes or mitochondrial maintenance.
E
Dependence locks inOnce the organelle lost the local gene, it depended on the host nucleus and protein-import machinery.

How Was The First Mitochondrion Created?

The first mitochondrion was not assembled as an organelle from loose molecules. It began as a living bacterial cell associated with a host lineage, then became an organelle through endosymbiosis and genetic integration.

First origin

An ancestral host lineage associated with an aerobic bacterial lineage. The relationship became stable and beneficial enough that the bacterium lived inside or tightly with the host.

Evolutionary event, not routine cell construction.

Transition

Genes were lost, transferred, or functionally replaced. Protein import deepened the dependency. Metabolic exchange made the merger advantageous.

Endosymbiont becomes organelle.

Modern biogenesis

Modern mitochondria grow, import proteins and lipids, replicate mitochondrial DNA, and divide from pre-existing mitochondria. Cells do not build mitochondria from zero.

Inheritance plus growth plus fission.

3. Autophagy, Mitophagy, And Macrophage

The similar sound comes from the eating root, but the mechanisms are not the same. Autophagy and mitophagy are pathways inside a cell. A macrophage is a whole immune cell.

Autophagy

Literal idea: self-eating.

Where: inside the cytoplasm of eukaryotic cells.

What is made: a temporary autophagosome assembled around cargo.

By whom: the cell's own autophagy machinery, mostly nuclear-encoded ATG-pathway proteins.

Mitophagy

Literal idea: mitochondria-eating.

Where: in the cytoplasm, at mitochondria marked for removal.

What is made: an autophagosome around a damaged or selected mitochondrion.

By whom: the same cell, using selective-autophagy receptors/adaptors and lysosomes.

Macrophage

Literal idea: big eater.

Where: tissues, immune sites, and inflammation contexts as a living immune cell.

What is made: a differentiated phagocytic cell, not an autophagosome.

By whom: the organism's hematopoietic and tissue-development systems; adult inflammatory macrophages often derive from blood monocytes, while many tissue-resident macrophages are embryonically seeded.

Autophagy Is A Temporary Recycling Route

An autophagosome is not inherited like a mitochondrion and is not a cell type like a macrophage. It is assembled when the cell needs to recycle material, survive starvation, remove damaged components, or reshape its internal contents.

1
TriggerNutrient stress, organelle damage, protein aggregates, developmental programs, infection, or quality-control signals activate the pathway.
↓
2
Phagophore nucleationA membrane grows around selected cargo. Membrane sources can include ER-associated sites and other membrane pools depending on context.
↓
3
Autophagosome closureThe double-membrane compartment seals cargo away from the rest of the cytosol.
↓
4
Lysosome fusionThe autophagosome fuses with lysosomal degradation machinery.
↓
5
RecyclingMacromolecules are broken down and useful building blocks return to metabolism.

Mitophagy Is Mitochondrial Quality Control

Damaged mitochondria are risky because they can lose membrane potential, disturb calcium handling, produce excess reactive oxygen species, fail at ATP production, or push death signaling. Mitophagy lets the cell remove the defective unit without destroying the whole cell.

1
Damage or selectionA mitochondrion loses function, becomes developmentally scheduled for removal, or is otherwise marked as excess or defective.
↓
2
MarkingIn a canonical pathway, PINK1 accumulates on a damaged mitochondrial outer membrane and Parkin-related ubiquitin signaling can help recruit autophagy machinery. Other receptor-mediated routes also exist.
↓
3
EngulfmentSelective autophagy receptors/adaptors connect the marked mitochondrion to the growing autophagosome membrane.
↓
4
Lysosomal digestionThe mitochondrion is delivered to lysosomes, degraded, and recycled.

4. "Plasm" Means Living Cell Material, But Protein Status Is More Precise

"Plasm" by itself is not a precise modern mechanism word. It appears in cytoplasm, nucleoplasm, protoplasm, plasma membrane, and plasmodesmata. The useful modern question is: how does each protein keep the right state and location in the crowded cell interior?

Word Meaning How it relates to protein state
Cytoplasm Cytosol plus organelles and internal structures outside the nucleus. Many proteins are translated, folded, transported, modified, concentrated, or degraded here.
Nucleoplasm The nuclear interior around chromatin and nuclear bodies. Contains transcription factors, polymerases, chromatin proteins, nuclear bodies, RNAs, and import/export machinery.
Protoplasm Older broad term for living cell contents. Useful historically, but too broad for mechanism.
Plasma membrane The lipid-protein cell boundary. Many proteins are kept in a membrane state by transmembrane helices, lipid anchors, trafficking, and retention.
Plasmodesmata Plant-cell channels through cell walls. Connect cytoplasms between neighboring plant cells.

How Proteins Keep Their State

Proteins do not merely float in a featureless liquid. Their status is maintained by sequence signals, transport systems, folding control, binding equilibria, post-translational modifications, phase behavior, and degradation.

LocationSignal peptides, mitochondrial targeting sequences, nuclear localization signals, transmembrane helices, peroxisomal targeting signals, vesicle routes, and retention systems.
FoldingChaperones, folding catalysts, redox conditions, quality-control checkpoints, and degradation pathways keep proteins from remaining misfolded.
BindingProteins can be free, bound to partners, built into complexes, attached to scaffolds, or held at membrane/cytoskeleton surfaces.
Chemical modificationPhosphorylation, acetylation, methylation, ubiquitination, oxidation, nucleotide binding, and lipidation alter activity, location, and lifetime.
Phase behaviorSome proteins remain dilute, some enter biomolecular condensates, some polymerize, some embed in membranes, and damaged ones may aggregate.
LifetimeThe ubiquitin-proteasome system, lysosomes, autophagy, and cell division dilution keep protein populations from persisting forever.

Cytoplasm Is Liquid, But Not Homogeneous

The cytoplasm is water-rich, yet crowded and organized. Proteins and RNAs experience local differences in concentration, pH, ions, ATP, redox state, membrane surface, cytoskeletal proximity, and stress condition.

Better phrase than "plasm status" Use protein state: where the protein is, what it is bound to, whether it is folded, how it is modified, which physical phase it occupies, and how long it will last.
  • A soluble enzyme can remain diffuse in the cytosol.
  • A receptor can stay embedded in a membrane through transmembrane domains.
  • A transcription factor can accumulate in the nucleus through a nuclear localization signal.
  • A mitochondrial protein can be translated in the cytosol and imported through TOM/TIM complexes.
  • An RNA-binding protein can move into a stress granule-like condensate under stress.
  • A misfolded protein can be refolded, degraded by proteasomes, or captured by autophagy if aggregates form.

5. "Chromatic" Here Means Chromatin

Chromatin is DNA plus histones plus non-histone proteins, RNAs, and regulatory factors. Its job is not just compaction. It packages DNA while controlling access for transcription, replication, repair, recombination, and chromosome segregation.

DNA double helixThe sequence stores genetic information, but naked DNA would be too long and too exposed.
NucleosomeAbout 147 base pairs wrap around histone proteins H2A, H2B, H3, and H4.
Beads on a stringNeighboring nucleosomes are connected by linker DNA; histone H1 can help organize higher compaction.
Domains and loopsRegions fold into regulatory neighborhoods so promoters, enhancers, silencers, and boundary elements can interact in 3D.
Mitotic chromosomeDuring cell division, chromatin condenses into a segregation-ready state.

Chromatin Is A Dynamic Packing System

The same DNA can be easy or hard to read depending on nucleosome placement, histone marks, chromatin remodelers, DNA methylation in some contexts, transcription-factor binding, loop organization, and nuclear neighborhood.

Open chromatinMore accessible nucleosomes and active regulatory marks make it easier for transcription factors and polymerases to reach DNA.
Compact chromatinDense packing and repressive marks make regions harder to access and often associated with gene silencing.
Looped regulationDistant DNA sites can be brought near one another so enhancers, promoters, silencers, and insulators work in 3D.
Mitotic condensationChromatin becomes highly compact for safe chromosome segregation; transcription is broadly reduced during this state.

How Chromatin Changes Without Changing DNA Sequence

The cell can change access to DNA without rewriting the letters. That is why chromatin belongs in the same mental category as membranes and cytoplasm: it is regulated physical organization.

Nucleosome movementRemodeling machines can slide, eject, or reposition nucleosomes.
Histone variantsReplacing standard histones can alter local chromatin properties.
Histone-tail marksAcetylation, methylation, phosphorylation, ubiquitination, and other marks recruit readers and influence accessibility.
DNA methylationIn many eukaryotic contexts, methylation helps define long-term regulatory states.
Architectural loopsProtein complexes can organize DNA into loops and domains.
Replication and repair reset pointsDNA copying and repair require temporary opening and later reassembly of chromatin.

One Integrated Model

The four questions are connected by one principle: a cell survives by managing inherited history and present-time organization at the same time.

IdentityThe nucleus stores and regulates most instructions.
EnergyMitochondria preserve a bacterial-descended energy module.
MaintenanceAutophagy and lysosomes recycle failed or excess parts.
Physical stateCytoplasm and nucleoplasm keep proteins in regulated locations and phases.
InheritanceChromatin packages DNA so it can be read, copied, repaired, silenced, or divided.
