# Cytoskeleton, Shape, Transport, And Division

The **cytoskeleton** is the cell's dynamic internal framework. It gives the cell shape, organizes cytoplasm, positions organelles, powers movement, and helps divide chromosomes. It is not a fixed skeleton like bone. It is a regulated network of polymers that can assemble, disassemble, push, pull, anchor, and serve as tracks.

OpenStax describes the cytoskeleton as protein fibers that maintain cell shape, secure organelles, allow cytoplasm and vesicles to move, and enable cell movement. NCBI Bookshelf's cytoskeleton, motor-protein, microtubule, intermediate-filament, and actin-myosin chapters add the key mechanical point: cytoskeletal filaments are not one material. They are three different polymer systems with different shapes, stiffness, polarity, motors, and failure modes.

## First Picture: Three Filament Materials

| Filament system | What it looks like | Diameter scale | Polarity | Main mechanical job |
|-----------------|--------------------|----------------|----------|---------------------|
| Actin filaments / microfilaments | Two twisted chains of actin beads | About 7 nm | Polar: plus and minus ends | Push membranes, form cortex, contract with myosin |
| Microtubules | A hollow tube made from tubulin protofilaments | About 25 nm | Polar: plus and minus ends | Long tracks, stiff struts, spindle, cilia/flagella |
| Intermediate filaments | Rope-like cable built from coiled-coil proteins | About 10 nm | Apolar: no plus/minus track direction | Resist stretching, tearing, and nuclear/tissue stress |

```
actin filament:
  thin twisted chain, good for pushing edges and contracting with myosin

microtubule:
  hollow pipe, good for long-range transport and chromosome movement

intermediate filament:
  braided rope, good for mechanical toughness
```

## Actin: A Thin Twisted Filament For Edges, Shape Change, And Contraction

Actin exists as **G-actin** monomers that polymerize into **F-actin** filaments. The filament behaves like a two-stranded helix: many actin subunits are arranged into a flexible, polar cable. The plus end and minus end are not equivalent, which lets the cell grow, shrink, cap, sever, branch, or bundle actin in controlled directions.

![Actin filament diagram showing a filament made from repeated actin subunits.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Actin_Filament_%28NIH_BioArt_626628%29.svg/1280px-Actin_Filament_%28NIH_BioArt_626628%29.svg.png)

*Figure 2. Actin looks like a thin twisted chain of repeated subunits. This geometry is why actin can form flexible networks, tight bundles, and contractile cables. Source: [Wikimedia Commons, Actin Filament (NIH BioArt 626628).svg](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Actin_Filament_%28NIH_BioArt_626628%29.svg).*

### How Actin Works

Actin's strength is local force. It is concentrated under the plasma membrane, where small changes in filament growth or myosin pulling can reshape the cell surface.

| Actin-associated process | Physical picture | Result |
|--------------------------|------------------|--------|
| Cell cortex | Dense actin mesh under the plasma membrane | The cell surface becomes mechanically supported but still flexible |
| Lamellipodium / crawling edge | Branched actin grows against the membrane | The front edge pushes forward |
| Filopodium / microvillus | Parallel actin bundle | Thin protrusion or stable membrane projection |
| Endocytosis | Actin assembly helps bend and pull membrane | Vesicle formation becomes easier |
| Cytokinesis | Actin-myosin ring contracts | Animal cell pinches into two daughter cells |
| Muscle or contractile bundle | Myosin pulls on actin | Filaments slide and the structure shortens |

One important actin trick is **polymerization pushing**:

```
plasma membrane
      |
      v
  + end of actin filament adds subunits
      |
      v
  growing filament presses membrane forward
      |
      v
  cell edge moves
```

That is different from myosin contraction. Actin can push by growing; actin can also be pulled by myosin.

## Microtubules: Hollow Tubes For Long-Range Organization

Microtubules are built from alpha/beta-tubulin dimers. Those dimers stack head-to-tail into protofilaments, and typical eukaryotic microtubules contain 13 protofilaments arranged around a hollow core. This makes a microtubule much stiffer than an actin filament.

![Microtubule structure diagram showing tubulin dimers, protofilaments, and the hollow tube.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Microtubule_Structure.svg/1280px-Microtubule_Structure.svg.png)

*Figure 3. A microtubule is a hollow tube assembled from tubulin dimers. The tube geometry makes it useful as a long cellular track and as a compression-resistant strut. Source: [Wikimedia Commons, Microtubule Structure.svg](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Microtubule_Structure.svg).*

### How Microtubules Work

Microtubules are cell-scale organizers. They often grow outward from a microtubule-organizing center near the centrosome in animal cells, with minus ends anchored inward and plus ends exploring outward.

```
cell center / centrosome
      |
      | minus ends often anchored here
      v
  microtubules radiate outward
      |
      | plus ends grow, shrink, search, and capture
      v
cell edge, organelles, vesicles, chromosomes
```

| Microtubule behavior | Meaning |
|----------------------|---------|
| Dynamic instability | A plus end can switch between growth and rapid shrinkage |
| Kinesin transport | Kinesins commonly move cargo toward plus ends |
| Dynein transport | Dynein commonly moves cargo toward minus ends |
| Mitotic spindle | Microtubules capture chromosomes and separate them |
| Cilia and flagella | Stable microtubule bundles bend to move fluid or propel cells |
| Organelle positioning | ER, Golgi, mitochondria, endosomes, and vesicles use microtubule organization |

The key mental image: microtubules are not just rods. They are **search-and-capture tracks**. They grow, shrink, and give motor proteins a directional road system.

## Intermediate Filaments: Rope-Like Cables For Toughness

Intermediate filaments are built differently from actin and microtubules. Their subunits form coiled-coil dimers, dimers associate into antiparallel tetramers, and tetramers pack into a rope-like cable. Because the tetramers are antiparallel, intermediate filaments are **apolar**. They do not make plus-end/minus-end tracks for kinesin, dynein, or myosin.

![Intermediate filament assembly diagram showing subunits forming coiled-coil and rope-like filament structure.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Intermediate_filament_sr.svg/1280px-Intermediate_filament_sr.svg.png)

*Figure 4. Intermediate filaments are best pictured as braided molecular rope. They are less about fast directed transport and more about resisting mechanical stress. Source: [Wikimedia Commons, Intermediate filament sr.svg](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Intermediate_filament_sr.svg).*

### How Intermediate Filaments Work

Intermediate filaments protect cells that are pulled, stretched, rubbed, or compressed.

| Intermediate-filament class | Where it matters | What failure looks like conceptually |
|-----------------------------|------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Keratins | Epithelial cells, skin, hair, nails | Tissue fragility, blistering, weak epithelial integrity |
| Vimentin | Many mesenchymal cells | Reduced resilience during migration, deformation, and wound response |
| Desmin | Muscle cells | Weak mechanical integration of contractile machinery |
| Neurofilaments | Neurons | Axon caliber and mechanical support problems |
| Nuclear lamins | Nuclear lamina under inner nuclear membrane | Nuclear fragility, altered nuclear shape, gene-regulation stress |

Actin and microtubules are excellent for dynamic motion, but they break and remodel frequently. Intermediate filaments are the durability layer.

## Motor Proteins: The Cytoskeleton Is Also A Railway System

| Motor | Track | Common direction or role | Useful picture |
|-------|-------|--------------------------|----------------|
| Kinesin | Microtubule | Often toward plus ends | Cargo carrier walking outward |
| Dynein | Microtubule | Often toward minus ends | Cargo carrier pulling inward; also cilia/flagella force |
| Myosin | Actin | Usually toward actin plus ends, depending on myosin type | Actin-based cargo movement or contraction |

```
microtubule track:
  minus end near center ------------------------- plus end near edge
       dynein often inward <--- cargo ---> kinesin often outward

actin track:
  local cortical network, bundles, ring, or muscle thin filament
       myosin walks along actin and can pull structures together
```

The cell therefore uses actin for local cortical mechanics, microtubules for long-range logistics, and intermediate filaments for tensile strength.

## Actin And Myosin Sliding: What Actually Slides?

The phrase "actin and myosin slide against each other" is easy to misunderstand. It does **not** mean actin shrinks. It does **not** mean myosin shrinks. It means myosin heads repeatedly grab actin, change shape, release, reset, and grab again. That repeated motor cycle pulls actin filaments past myosin filaments.

![Sliding filament theory diagram showing actin, myosin, tropomyosin, troponin, ATP, and the myosin head cycle.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Sliding_Filament_Theory.svg/1280px-Sliding_Filament_Theory.svg.png)

*Figure 5. In the sliding-filament model, myosin heads repeatedly bind actin and perform power strokes. The filaments keep roughly the same length, but their overlap changes. Source: [Wikimedia Commons, Sliding Filament Theory.svg](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Sliding_Filament_Theory.svg).*

### The Sarcomere Picture

In skeletal muscle, actin and myosin are organized into repeating units called **sarcomeres**.

```
RELAXED SARCOMERE

Z disc                  M line                  Z disc
|                       |                       |
| actin ---->      <----|---->      <---- actin |
|        \        /     |     \        /        |
|         myosin thick filament centered        |
|                                                |
Z discs far apart; overlap exists but is limited


CONTRACTED SARCOMERE

Z disc             M line             Z disc
|                  |                  |
| actin ----><-----|-----><---- actin |
|       \    /     |     \    /       |
|        myosin thick filament        |
|                                     |
Z discs closer together; actin has slid inward
```

What changes:

| Feature | During contraction |
|---------|--------------------|
| Actin filament length | Stays roughly the same |
| Myosin filament length | Stays roughly the same |
| Overlap between actin and myosin | Increases |
| Distance between Z discs | Decreases |
| I band and H zone | Shrink |
| A band | Stays about the same width because myosin length does not change |

So the best image is:

```
myosin heads = tiny hands
actin filament = rope

cycle:
  grab actin
  pull
  release
  reset using ATP
  grab farther along actin
  pull again
```

### Cross-Bridge Cycle In Plain Language

| Step | What happens | Visual meaning |
|------|--------------|----------------|
| 1. Calcium exposes actin binding sites | In muscle, Ca2+ moves regulatory proteins so myosin can bind actin | The rope becomes grabbable |
| 2. Myosin attaches to actin | A myosin head forms a cross-bridge | Hand grabs rope |
| 3. Power stroke | Myosin changes shape and pulls actin toward the sarcomere center | Hand pulls rope inward |
| 4. ATP binds myosin | Myosin detaches from actin | Hand lets go |
| 5. ATP hydrolysis resets myosin | Myosin head returns to a cocked high-energy position | Hand reaches again |
| 6. Cycle repeats | Many myosin heads work asynchronously | The filament slides smoothly |

The "sliding" is therefore not passive slipping. It is active molecular walking powered by ATP.

### Same Principle Outside Muscle

Non-muscle cells also use actin and myosin II. The arrangement is smaller and less regular than a sarcomere, but the logic is similar.

```
cytokinesis in an animal cell:

cell cortex under membrane
        |
        v
actin-myosin contractile ring forms around cell equator
        |
        v
myosin pulls actin filaments around the ring
        |
        v
ring tightens
        |
        v
membrane pinches inward
        |
        v
two daughter cells separate
```

## Putting The Three Systems Together

| Question | Actin | Microtubules | Intermediate filaments |
|----------|-------|--------------|------------------------|
| How should I picture it? | Thin twisted chain or local mesh | Hollow tube / long track | Braided rope |
| Is it polar? | Yes | Yes | No |
| Does it have motor proteins? | Myosins | Kinesins and dyneins | No major walking motors |
| Main movement style | Push membrane, contract, change shape | Move cargo, search/capture, segregate chromosomes | Resist stretch and mechanical damage |
| Main location feel | Cell cortex, protrusions, contractile rings, muscle thin filaments | Radial cytoplasm, spindle, cilia/flagella | Across cytoplasm, tissue junctions, nuclear lamina |
| What breaks if it fails? | Shape change, crawling, contraction, cytokinesis | Transport, mitosis, cilia, organelle positioning | Mechanical resilience, tissue integrity, nuclear support |

## Cytoskeleton And Organelles

The cytoskeleton positions organelles and routes cargo.

| Organelle or cargo | Cytoskeletal relationship |
|--------------------|---------------------------|
| Mitochondria | Moved along microtubules and actin; positioned near energy demand |
| Golgi apparatus | Often organized near microtubule-organizing regions |
| ER | Extended through cytoplasm along cytoskeletal structures |
| Vesicles | Transported by kinesin, dynein, and myosin motors |
| Nucleus | Mechanically linked to cytoskeleton through nuclear envelope systems and supported by lamins |
| Chromosomes | Captured, aligned, and segregated by spindle microtubules |

Mitochondria are a particularly good example. A cell can produce ATP in mitochondria, but if mitochondria are not positioned where ATP and calcium buffering are needed, function still suffers.

## Animal And Plant Cytoskeletons

Animal and plant cells both have cytoskeletons, but they use them differently.

Animal cells often rely on flexible shape changes, crawling, contraction, cilia, and tissue junctions. Many animal cells have centrosomes with centrioles that organize microtubules.

Plant cells have rigid cell walls, but their cytoskeleton is still essential. Microtubules help guide cellulose deposition, actin supports cytoplasmic streaming, and plant cells build spindles for division. OpenStax notes that plant cells have microtubules, intermediate filaments, and microfilaments, but not centrosomes in the same typical animal-cell sense.

## Summary

- The cytoskeleton is a dynamic network, not a fixed scaffold.
- Actin looks like a thin twisted filament. It builds local networks and bundles, pushes membranes by polymerization, and contracts when myosin pulls on it.
- Microtubules look like hollow tubes. They are stiff polar tracks for kinesin/dynein transport, mitotic spindles, cilia/flagella, and organelle positioning.
- Intermediate filaments look like braided rope. They are apolar, tough, and specialized for mechanical resilience rather than fast transport.
- In actin-myosin sliding, neither filament shortens. Myosin heads repeatedly grab, pull, release, and reset, increasing actin-myosin overlap and shortening the larger structure.
- Mitochondria, ER, Golgi, vesicles, nuclei, and chromosomes all depend on cytoskeletal organization.
